Cat Bite Infection

Cat bites, especially from stray or feral cats, carry a high risk of infection due to the bacteria in their mouths. While house cats are often vaccinated, stray cats may carry diseases like rabies or tetanus. Left untreated, infections from cat bites can spread, potentially leading to septicemia (blood poisoning) or other severe complications.

Signs of Infection

Common symptoms include redness, swelling, warmth, and bumps or blisters around the bite. Severe signs include pus, red streaks, swollen lymph nodes, fever, fatigue, or loss of feeling near the wound

Precautions and Next Steps

  • Always clean the bite thoroughly and monitor for signs of infection.
  • Seek immediate medical care if bitten by a stray or unvaccinated cat or if symptoms of infection or rabies appear.
  • Contact animal control for stray cats rather than attempting to capture them yourself.

Prompt treatment is crucial to prevent serious complications from cat bites.

Signs of Infection

Common Symptoms

  • Redness

  • Swelling

  • Warmth

  • Bumps or blisters around the bite


Severe Symptoms

  • Pus

  • Red Streaks

  • Swollen lymph nodes

  • Fever

  • Fever

  • Fatigue

  • Numbness near the wound

If you notice signs of infection, such as inflammation, pain, redness, or swelling, seek medical care.

When to Seek Medical Help

  • Serious symptoms of infection appear, such as fever, chills, pus, or swollen lymph nodes.
  • The wound is deep, large, or won’t stop bleeding.
  • The biting cat behaves aggressively, acts strangely, or is unvaccinated.
  • You haven’t had a tetanus shot in the last five years.
  • Cause loss of function (e.g., inability to bend fingers).
  • You have a weakened immune system.

Also seek medical help if you:

  • Don’t remember your last tetanus shot.
  • Feel weak, disoriented, or faint.
  • Have a fever.

Treatment Steps

  • 1. Wash the wound with soap and water.

  • 2. Use antibacterial ointment.

  • 3. Cover with a sterile bandage.
